# Tomatito F.A.Q.

Honest answers, with science where it exists. Ratings:
**strong** = many studies, well-replicated.
**weak** = small studies or contested.
**none** = no good evidence; popular folklore.

## Why 25 minutes of focus and 5 of break?

There is no research that picked 25/5 over 50/10 or 90/20.

Francesco Cirillo coined the Pomodoro Technique in the late 1980s with that ratio because his kitchen timer was tomato-shaped (_pomodoro_ in Italian) and the duration felt right to him. What research does support is the rhythm: timed focus followed by deliberate rest beats unstructured work. The exact numbers matter less than the cycle.

If 25/5 doesn't fit you, change it. 50/10 and 90/20 are common alternatives. Pick a duration you'll actually do, not the one that sounds disciplined.

## What does research strongly support?

**Task switching is expensive.** _Mark, Gonzalez & Harris (2005)_ observed knowledge workers and found interrupted tasks took meaningfully longer to resume; workers commonly performed two or three intervening tasks before returning to the original. The often-cited "23 minutes to refocus" is a paraphrase of the underlying finding.

**Attention residue is real.** _Leroy (2009)_ showed that performance on a new task is impaired when attention remains on the previous one, especially under time pressure. Implication: cleanly closing a focus block matters more than its specific length.

**Real breaks restore attention; pseudo-breaks do not.** Attention Restoration Theory (_Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989; Berman, Jonides & Kaplan, 2008_) shows environments that offer "soft fascination" — nature, water, fire — restore directed attention. Scrolling a feed or switching to another work task does not. This is one of the most important findings in the literature for break design.

**Movement during breaks helps.** Multiple studies (_Buman & King, 2010_ onwards) show physical activity during breaks improves subsequent cognitive performance versus sedentary breaks. Even a minute of walking helps.

**Pre-committing to a time helps.** _Gollwitzer (1999)_ showed implementation intentions ("I will focus from 14:00 to 14:25") increase the probability of doing it. Starting the timer is itself a useful intervention.

**Micro-breaks reduce fatigue.** _Trougakos et al. (2014)_ and a 2022 systematic review (_Albulescu et al._) support short breaks for cognitively demanding work.

## What does NOT work as well as people claim?

**The specific 25/5 ratio.** Studies on the Pomodoro Technique exist (_Costales et al. 2020_ and others) but are small, methodologically limited, and mostly measure self-reported productivity. There is no strong evidence that 25/5 is optimal versus, say, 50/10 or 90/20. Your personal optimum varies by task, individual, and time of day.

**The 90-minute "ultradian" cycle.** Kleitman's Basic Rest Activity Cycle was a sleep finding. Its extension to waking productivity is widely repeated but empirically thin.

**"Deep work" hour caps** ("humans can only do 4 hours of deep work per day"). Popularised by Cal Newport. More anecdotal observation than measured limit.

**"21 days to form a habit."** _Maxwell Maltz_ observation, never research. _Lally et al. (2010)_ found actual habit formation ranged from 18 to 254 days depending on the behaviour.

## What about the tick sound during focus?

Mixed evidence. Some studies suggest a faint metronome aids time awareness without distraction; others show any auditory load impairs deep cognitive work. Off by default; try it both ways and notice what works for you. The one in Tomatito is intentionally low-frequency and quiet.

## The 20-20-20 rule for screens?

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet (6 metres) away for 20 seconds. American Optometric Association consensus, not strongly RCT-supported but widely accepted to reduce eye strain and dryness.

## What helps most in practice?

- **Real breaks.** Stand up, look out a window, get water. Not another screen.
- **Movement.** Even a minute of walking helps.
- **Closing one task before starting the next.** Leftover attention slows you down.
- **Picking a duration you'll actually do**, not the one that sounds disciplined.
- **Strict mode** when the temptation to pause is high. Each pause is a self-interruption that incurs the resumption cost.
