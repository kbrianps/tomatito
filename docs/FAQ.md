# Tomatito F.A.Q.

Honest answers, with science where it exists. Ratings:
**strong** = many studies, well-replicated.
**weak** = small studies or contested.
**none** = no good evidence; popular folklore.

> Tomatito is opinionated about telling you when something is folklore. If a section says "weak" or "none," try it for yourself, but don't believe anyone (including us) who tells you it's settled.

## Why 25 minutes of focus and 5 of break?

There is no research that picked 25/5 over 50/10 or 90/20. **Strength of evidence: none.**

Francesco Cirillo coined the Pomodoro Technique in the late 1980s with that ratio because his kitchen timer was tomato-shaped (_pomodoro_ in Italian) and the duration felt right to him. What research does support is the rhythm: timed focus followed by deliberate rest beats unstructured work. The exact numbers matter less than the cycle.

If 25/5 doesn't fit you, change it. 50/10 and 90/20 are common alternatives. Pick a duration you'll actually do, not the one that sounds disciplined.

## How do I know which focus length suits me?

A practical rule: the right block is the longest one you can finish without checking your phone, and the shortest one that gets you past the warm-up. For most coding, writing, and design work that lands somewhere between 25 and 60 minutes; for shallow tasks (email, admin) shorter is usually fine; for deep problems where you need to load a big mental model, 50 to 90 is common.

Try one length for a week, then change one variable. Don't optimise blocks while also changing your sleep, caffeine, and goals.

## What does research strongly support?

**Task switching is expensive.** [Mark, Gonzalez & Harris (2005)](https://doi.org/10.1145/1054972.1055017) observed knowledge workers and found interrupted tasks took meaningfully longer to resume; workers commonly performed two or three intervening tasks before returning to the original. The often-cited "23 minutes to refocus" is a paraphrase of the underlying finding.

**Attention residue is real.** [Leroy (2009)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.obhdp.2009.04.002) showed that performance on a new task is impaired when attention remains on the previous one, especially under time pressure. Implication: cleanly closing a focus block matters more than its specific length.

**Real breaks restore attention; pseudo-breaks do not.** Attention Restoration Theory ([Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/experience-of-nature/A8283C9CA85F71F8DC1A6C4E64C7BF11); [Berman, Jonides & Kaplan, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02225.x)) shows environments that offer "soft fascination" (nature, water, fire) restore directed attention. Scrolling a feed or switching to another work task does not. This is one of the most important findings in the literature for break design.

**Movement during breaks helps.** Multiple studies ([Buman & King, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1177/1559827610375532) onwards) show physical activity during breaks improves subsequent cognitive performance versus sedentary breaks. Even a minute of walking helps.

**Pre-committing to a time helps.** [Gollwitzer (1999)](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493) showed implementation intentions ("I will focus from 14:00 to 14:25") increase the probability of doing it. Starting the timer is itself a useful intervention.

**Micro-breaks reduce fatigue.** [Trougakos et al. (2014)](https://doi.org/10.5465/amj.2011.1072) and a 2022 systematic review ([Albulescu et al.](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0272460)) support short breaks for cognitively demanding work.

## What does NOT work as well as people claim?

**The specific 25/5 ratio.** Studies on the Pomodoro Technique exist ([Costales et al., 2020](https://doi.org/10.1145/3411170.3411258) and others) but are small, methodologically limited, and mostly measure self-reported productivity. There is no strong evidence that 25/5 is optimal versus, say, 50/10 or 90/20. Your personal optimum varies by task, individual, and time of day.

**The 90-minute "ultradian" cycle.** [Kleitman's Basic Rest Activity Cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_rest%E2%80%93activity_cycle) was a sleep finding. Its extension to waking productivity is widely repeated but empirically thin.

**"Deep work" hour caps** ("humans can only do 4 hours of deep work per day"). Popularised by Cal Newport. More anecdotal observation than measured limit.

**"21 days to form a habit."** Maxwell Maltz observation, never research. [Lally et al. (2010)](https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsp.674) found actual habit formation ranged from 18 to 254 days depending on the behaviour.

## What about caffeine?

**Strong** for short-term alertness and reaction time, especially when sleep-deprived. [Smith (2002)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0278-6915(02)00096-0) and many follow-ups confirm modest cognitive benefits at moderate doses (40 to 200 mg). It does not, however, restore attention the way a real break does.

Practical guidance from sleep research: caffeine has a 5 to 7 hour half-life. A coffee at 16:00 still leaves about half its dose in your system at midnight. If you sleep poorly, that's your first variable to test, not your focus block length.

## Music while focusing?

**Mixed.** [Kämpfe et al. (2011)](https://doi.org/10.1177/0305735610376261) meta-analysis: background music has small effects in either direction depending on task and listener. Roughly: lyrics tend to hurt language-heavy work; instrumental or familiar tracks tend to be neutral or mildly helpful for repetitive tasks.

If music helps you start focusing, that's a real benefit even if it doesn't measurably improve the work itself. Just notice whether you're listening or working.

## What's strict mode for, really?

Each pause is a self-interruption. Even a 30-second pause incurs the resumption cost we discussed under "attention residue." Strict mode removes the pause button during focus periods so the cheap escape is gone; you either keep going or you reset.

Use it when you keep catching yourself pausing the timer to check things. Do **not** use it as punishment. If you need pauses (medical, caretaking, your concentration is genuinely shot), keep it off and don't feel guilty.

## Why does my break feel unproductive?

Probably because you're spending it on another screen. Attention Restoration Theory predicts this directly: looking at your phone is not a break for the same parts of the brain you just used. A 5-minute walk, a window with sky in it, water, food, or just standing up and stretching all qualify. Doom-scrolling doesn't.

The good news: even small changes help. One study ([Berman et al., 2008](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02225.x)) measured restoration after a 50-minute walk in nature; effects were detectable but modest. You don't need an hour in a forest. You need to not look at TikTok.

## What about the tick sound during focus?

Mixed evidence. Some studies suggest a faint metronome aids time awareness without distraction; others show any auditory load impairs deep cognitive work. Off by default; try it both ways and notice what works for you. The one in Tomatito is intentionally low-frequency and quiet.

## The 20-20-20 rule for screens?

Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet (6 metres) away for 20 seconds. American Optometric Association consensus, not strongly RCT-supported but widely accepted to reduce eye strain and dryness. Tomatito does not enforce it; its 5-minute breaks already give you the chance, if you actually take them.

## I keep getting distracted mid-block. What do I do?

The classic implementation-intention approach ([Gollwitzer, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493)) generalises here: pre-commit to what you'll do _when_ a distraction arrives, not just whether you'll resist it. For example: "If I think of an unrelated task, I'll write it on the scratchpad and keep focusing." This works because the decision is already made; you just execute.

Open tabs and notifications matter more than willpower. Closing the chat client and silencing your phone is doing more for you than the timer is.

## Do streaks actually help?

**Weak.** Streaks borrow from habit-formation research ([Lally et al., 2010](https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsp.674)) and gamification research, but the specific "don't break the chain" mechanic is mostly anecdotal. They can backfire if you start optimising for the streak rather than the work; we built the streak so today does not break it unless your minutes are short, so a missed afternoon doesn't punish you for things outside your control.

## What helps most in practice?

- **Real breaks.** Stand up, look out a window, get water. Not another screen.
- **Movement.** Even a minute of walking helps.
- **Closing one task before starting the next.** Leftover attention slows you down.
- **Picking a duration you'll actually do**, not the one that sounds disciplined.
- **Strict mode** when the temptation to pause is high. Each pause is a self-interruption that incurs the resumption cost.
- **Sleep before tools.** No focus app fixes a 5-hour night.
